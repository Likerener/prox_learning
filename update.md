## 0. The hypothesis we are testing

A learned manipulation policy that consumes **RGB cameras + 29 SPAD-style
8 × 8 proximity sensors** ("franka_skin") should outperform an
otherwise-identical policy that consumes RGB alone, especially in the
**close-range / pre-grasp / cluttered** regime where RGB struggles.

Headline metric: success rate of "pick the target object and place it on
the receptacle" on held-out houses, with `task_info.robot_contact` rate
as the secondary metric for the close-approach signal.

## 3. Architecture (`pla/policy.py`, `pla/proximity_encoder.py`)

ACT-style CVAE policy, mirrors `submodules/act/detr/models/detr_vae.py`
exactly except for the proximity branch.

- **Image branch:** two ResNet-18 backbones (one per camera), shared
  input projection to hidden_dim = 512.
- **Proximity branch:** per-sensor MLP (Linear 64 → 128 → 128) shared
  across all 29 sensors, then a per-link learned positional embedding, then
  concatenated into the transformer encoder context as 29 extra tokens.
- **Language branch:** frozen CLIP ViT-B-32 text embedding (512-d), one
  embedding per trajectory's instruction string, concatenated as one
  additional token.
- **CVAE:** action sequence + qpos + CLS through a 4-layer encoder to
  produce `(mu, logvar)` of a 32-d latent z, decoded back through the main
  transformer along with the context tokens.
- **Heads:** `Linear(hidden_dim, action_dim=8)` for actions (7 arm + 1
  normalised gripper {0, 1}), `Linear(hidden_dim, 1)` for is_pad.
- **Loss:** L1 on actions + KL(q(z|x) || N(0,I)) with `kl_weight = 10`.
- **Optimizer:** AdamW, lr = 1e-5, weight_decay = 1e-4.
- **Param count:** 96.59 M trainable.
- **Masked links:** `mask_links=("link2",)` by default. Link-2 sensors are
  occluded by the robot's own elbow geometry and produce spurious
  near-saturation readings; zeroing them is a hard structural fix surfaced
  by the proximity audit.

**Configuration knobs that have been ablated in this project:**

- `use_proximity` ∈ {True, False} — PLA vs VLM-only baseline.
- `use_language` ∈ {True, False} — established that language helps; all
  medium-pilot runs use language on.
- `mask_links` ∈ {("link2",), ()} — link2-mask is the default; no-mask is
  a control.
- `chunk_size` ∈ {20, 100} — was hardcoded at 100 until this week; CLI
  flag added in `pla/train.py` for the k=20 ablation.
- `gripper_classifier` ∈ {True, False} — **new this week**: switches the
  gripper head from L1 regression to 2-way cross-entropy classification.

## 6. The diagnosed failure mode

Both PLA and VLM-only close the gripper at almost exactly the same dataset-typical
frame (~48), regardless of where the EE actually is. The medium_pla_v1 run on
house 19 reached TCP-to-pickup 2.57 cm at frame 31 — but still fired the
gripper at frame 47, by which point the EE had retreated to ~11 cm.

This is a closed-loop conditioning failure: ACT predicts a 100-frame chunk
from the initial observation and plays it open-loop. Proximity sensors carry
information about EE-to-object range, but with chunked open-loop playback
that information cannot affect gripper-close timing because the chunk was
committed long before proximity values become informative.

This is now documented in the auto-memory as `eval-chunk-timing-memorization`
so future sessions don't redo the diagnosis.

## 7. Fix attempts

### 7.1 Temporal ensembling at inference (done)

`pla/eval_policy.py` — every step, predict a fresh chunk; current action is
the exp-weighted mean over alive predictions targeting the current step.
Controlled by `PLA_TEMP_AGG_OFF` and `PLA_TEMP_AGG_M`. Default on.

**Result:** the memorisation pattern broke — gripper close frame mean
shifted from 48 to 82, std grew from ~3 to 28 — but reach got worse
(TCP min mean 18.9 → 26.5 cm) and success stayed at 0/10. The ensembled
action across a chunk that was never trained to be sampled mid-flight is
just noisier.

### 7.2 Shorter training-time chunk (k=20)

`pla/train.py --chunk_size 20`. Same configuration as medium_pla_v1 except
the training-time chunk length, so every prediction is conditioned on a
much fresher observation and closed-loop inference should be
in-distribution.

**First fire crashed** at step ~150 with a DataLoader-worker SIGKILL — the
system OOM-killer reaped a worker while the parallel smoke was also running.
Restarted with `num_workers=1`.

**At step 35k / 50k:** 0/10 success, contact 0/10, TCP min mean 36.4 cm
(worse than baseline), gripper closes in only 1/10 episodes. The
short-chunk lever is trending the wrong way on reach. Still running to 50k
for completeness.

### 7.3 Binary gripper classifier head (1c) — running

The gripper-close memorisation is consistent with an L1-regression failure
mode: gripper target is essentially binary ({0.0, 1.0} after normalising
{0, 255}), and L1 minimises the median, which on a {0, 1}-valued sequence
collapses to "predict the mean opening frame and ignore the input".
Architectural fix: route gripper through a 2-way cross-entropy classifier,
keep arm dims on L1.

Implementation:
- `pla/policy.py`: `PLAConfig.gripper_classifier` (default False); when
  True, add `gripper_head = Linear(hidden_dim, 2)`. `PLAPolicy.forward`
  splits the loss (L1 on dims [:7], CE on dim 7) and at inference fills the
  gripper dimension with `argmax(logits).float()` so the rest of the eval
  stack is untouched.
- `pla/train.py`: `--gripper_classifier`, `--gripper_ce_weight` CLI flags.

Smoke import test: forward + backward + inference all clean, param count
96.63 M (1024 new params).

`medium_pla_grip1c_v1_20260514_114550`. Step 1000 numbers: train loss 2.33
(l1 0.20, grip_ce 0.44, kl 0.17), val loss 1.04 (l1 0.38, grip_ce 0.55).
The classifier head is learning a discrete close decision. ETA finish late
tonight under GPU contention with k=20.

| Stat | Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|

| grip-close frame: mean / median / std | 48.9 / 50.0 / 9.3 | matches the policy's 48.4 to within 1 frame |
| grip-close frame: p10–p90 | [37, 60] | demonstrator IS varied, just on a narrow window |

| TCP-to-pickup at close: fraction < 5 cm | **64.7 %** | most grasps happen close to the target |
| Pearson r(close_frame, TCP_at_close) | **−0.179** | weak — closer demos close slightly later, but the link is loose |

What this tells us:

- The **demonstrator is fine**. ~65 % of closes happen within 5 cm of the
  pickup; the average is 4.3 cm. The dataset does not "always close at
  frame 48 regardless of TCP".
- **But the variance is narrow**: std 9.3 frames around mean 48.9 across
  343 trajectories. The L1-optimal prediction for that distribution is
  "close at frame 49" — which is exactly what the policy learns and
  exactly what kills the eval. With more variance in the data (or a
  classification head, not L1 regression), the policy would have to commit
  to *when* rather than collapse to the mean.
- The 1c training (binary CE classifier on gripper) is the right fix on
  this evidence.

## 10b.7 Per-house TCP-at-close correlation — **🔴 THE HEADLINE DIAGNOSTIC**

This is the single most important diagnostic in the document, because it
isolates whether the proximity-→-close-time signal exists *anywhere* in
the demonstrator data, even if it is destroyed by dataset-wide pooling.

For each of the 82 houses, we have between 1 and 5 demonstration
trajectories. For each trajectory we know:

- the frame at which the demonstrator first commanded the gripper to
  close, and
- the TCP-to-pickup distance at that exact frame.

If the demonstrator were *using proximity* to decide when to close, the
within-house correlation between close-frame and TCP-at-close would be
strongly negative on average: when the EE arrives early (closer to the
object sooner), the demonstrator closes earlier. The pooled correlation
might still be weak because intercepts and trajectory lengths vary by
house, but the per-house signal should be clean.

What we actually find (`pla/per_house_corr.py`, results in
`analysis_output/per_house_corr/`):

| Statistic | Value |
|---|---|
| Pooled Pearson r over all 343 trajectories | **−0.179** |
| Number of houses with ≥3 trajectories (used for within-house r) | 66 |
| Within-house r — mean | **−0.076** |
| Within-house r — median | **−0.160** |
| Within-house r — std | 0.39 |
| Fisher-z aggregated r | **−0.082** |
| Houses with within-house r < −0.3 | **39%** (26 / 66) |
| Houses with within-house r > +0.3 | **32%** (21 / 66) |

The Fisher-z aggregated correlation, which is the right way to average
correlations, is **−0.08** — even weaker than the pooled correlation in
absolute value. And the sign of the within-house r is essentially split
50/50: 39% of houses say "closer EE → earlier close" but 32% of houses
say the opposite.

This means the close-time decision in the demonstration data is **not
driven by EE-to-object distance, at either the within-house or the pooled
level**. The demonstrator closes the gripper on a near-fixed motion-time
schedule, not on a proximity signal.

### Why this is the most important finding

We have been chasing architectural fixes — temporal ensembling, shorter
chunks, binary CE gripper head, single-house overfit — all premised on
the belief that the demonstrator's data contains a learnable
proximity-→-close-time signal, and the policy is failing to extract it.

This diagnostic falsifies that premise. The demonstrator itself does not
use proximity to decide when to close. The data has no TCP→close-time
signal at the within-house or pooled level. **No architecture, however
well-designed, can extract a signal that is not in the data.**

That is consistent with:
- the oracle (privileged state, no vision) also failing 0/10 (§10c),
- the 1-house overfit train collapsing to L1 0.025 without producing
  better grasp behaviour (§4 row 10),
- 9/10 trajectories of the k=20 step-50k smoke never firing the gripper
  at all (the policy has learned the motion-time schedule, which gives no
  signal at inference because the simulator is not playing back the
  demonstrator's specific trajectory).

### What needs to change

To make PLA succeed, the demonstration data must contain a close-time
signal that is **driven by EE-to-object distance** rather than by motion
time. Two clean paths:

1. **Re-record demonstrations** with the planner closing the gripper
   when `task_info.robot_contact` first fires or when TCP-to-pickup
   drops below some threshold. The planner already knows the pickup
   pose in ground truth, so this is a one-line change in the demo
   policy. ~16 h of data collection to repopulate the medium dataset.
2. **Modify the existing demonstrations** post-hoc: shift each
   trajectory's gripper close to the frame at which TCP first dropped
   below 5 cm. Doesn't require re-collection; ~30 min of CPU. Loses any
   demonstrations where TCP never dropped below 5 cm (some episodes
   already do this — those would be dropped from training).

### 10c.3 What this changes

We had been narrowing in on "the policy has good encoders but a bad
gripper head" (→ 1c) and "the policy has good encoders but bad chunk
length" (→ k=20). The oracle result says **neither of those is the
limiting factor**. With ground-truth state, the same trained-with-L1,
chunked-playback recipe still produces the same memorisation pattern, on
the same houses, with even worse reach.

This is consistent with the §10b diagnostic — the training data has only
±9 frames of variance around mean close 48.9, so an L1-regression policy
of any architecture will collapse to that mean. The vision-only PLA gets a
bit closer than the oracle on reach because (we suspect) the visual cues
on close-range approach inject some last-frame variance the oracle does
not get from joint state alone, but neither model has enough close-time
signal to learn from.

**Implication for the 4 pm meeting:**
1. The bottleneck is not the encoder choice (PLA vs VLM-only vs Oracle).
2. The bottleneck is the action/loss representation. 1c is still the right
   first experiment but on this evidence the close-time loss alone may not
   be enough — the training data may genuinely not encode "TCP→close-time"
   strongly enough for any policy to recover it.
3. The clean test for that is: re-collect (or re-filter) demonstrations
   such that gripper-close frame correlates strongly with TCP-to-pickup
   distance. Today's data has Pearson r = −0.18 (very weak); even a
   discrete classifier head will struggle to learn from that.

## 10d. Overnight vanilla-ACT reproduction test (2026-05-15)

Most direct test of the architecture's ability to fit a single trajectory.

### 10d.1 Setup

1. Mug datagen produced **1 successful trajectory** in house_1 (planner can't
   find a second valid configuration; `HouseInvalidForTask` after every retry
   even with `max_allowed_sequential_task_sampler_failures=2000`).
2. `scripts/duplicate_one_trajectory.py` replicated that one trajectory 250×
   in a new dataset directory:
   - h5 written with `traj_0 … traj_249` (deep-copied).
   - For `i ≥ 1`, perturbed `obs/extra/obj_start[:, :3]` with Gaussian noise
     (σ=2 cm xy, σ=0.5 cm z) per duplicate. Action sequences unchanged.
   - MP4s hardlinked, so RGB is byte-identical across all 250 duplicates.
3. Trained vanilla ACT (`--use_proximity false --use_language false`) for
   30k steps. Wandb run name: `vanilla_act_mug_dup250_20260515_012042`.

### 10d.2 Training-side result — converges cleanly

| Step | Train L1 | Val L1 | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| 100 | 0.219 | — | — |
| 1k | 0.120 | 0.186 | 0.066 |
| 5k | 0.033 | 0.063 | 0.030 |
| 10k | 0.021 | 0.029 | 0.008 |
| 15k | 0.017 | 0.025 | 0.008 |
| 20k | 0.012 | 0.018 | 0.006 |
| 25k | 0.011 | 0.013 | 0.002 |
| **30k** | **0.009** | **0.013** | **0.004** |

This is the cleanest training curve we've seen anywhere in this project — 12×
drop in train L1, val tracking it, gap < 0.005 at convergence.

### 10d.3 Behaviour-side result — does not perform the task

In-sim smokes against fresh house_1 resets (same seed 2028 as everything else):

| Ckpt | TCP min mean | Best single | Grip close frame | Contact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 5k | 56.5 cm (n=1) | 56.5 cm | never | 0/1 |
| 10k | 56.4 cm (n=1) | 56.4 cm | never | 0/1 |
| 15k | 39.6 cm (n=3) | 20.2 cm | never | 0/3 |
| 20k | 43.0 cm (n=2) | 30.1 cm | frame 1 (1/2) | 0/2 |
| **30k** | **37.5 cm (n=3)** | 22.1 cm | **frame 1-18 (3/3)** | 0/3 |

The demonstrator closes at frame ~48 with TCP at ~4 cm. The trained
vanilla-ACT policy at step 30k closes at frame 1-18 with TCP at 22-57 cm.
**Loss is converged. Behaviour is not.**

### 10d.4 Likely cause

We hardlinked the MP4 files across the 250 duplicates, so the policy saw
literally byte-identical RGB across the entire dataset. When the simulator
generates a fresh house_1 with even small variations (lighting, mug
orientation, robot starting joint state), the policy is OOD on the very
first frame and cannot recover. This is consistent with the convex of
findings throughout the project:

- 343 trajectories (medium pilot): 0/10 success
- 5 trajectories from house_1 only (overfit): train L1 0.025 but no eval
- **1 trajectory × 250 duplicates: train L1 0.009 but still no in-sim
  performance**

At every dataset size, the architecture fits the loss landscape it sees but
**does not generalise to any sim-fresh observation**. The §10b.7 per-house
correlation finding said the demonstrator-side signal is absent; this
reproduction test says even when we control the demonstrator-side signal
perfectly (one trajectory, exactly known), the policy-side mapping from RGB
to actions is brittle.

### 10d.5 What needs to change

1. **Real visual variation in training data.** The duplicate-with-hardlinked-
   MP4s approach was always going to fail this test. The next attempt needs
   the simulator to actually re-render the trajectory N times with small
   pose perturbations on the camera or scene, so the policy sees a true
   visual distribution it must collapse to one action sequence.
2. **Or: a different action-space.** L1 regression on a 100-frame joint-pos
   chunk is unforgiving on close-range manipulation. Delta actions or
   relative-to-TCP waypoints would be more invariant.
3. **The planner's failure to generate variation in house_1 is itself
   informative.** It means even a perfect data pipeline is gated on the
   planner's IK reliability. A house_1-friendly task or a more permissive
   planner is needed before any of these reproduction tests can scale.

## 11. What this means for the next call

**Ruled out:**
1. "The proximity encoder is not learning anything" — both PLA and VLM-only
   fail in the same way, so the encoder is not the locus of the failure.
2. "PLA learns proximity but doesn't act on it because we infer in chunks" —
   per-step ensembling and shorter training-time chunks both fail to recover
   success and instead make reach worse.

**Most likely remaining cause:** the gripper-close decision is memorised at
the training-loss level, independent of inference protocol; fixing it
requires the loss or the head to actually treat gripper close as a
discrete event. That is the 1c training in flight.

**If 1c also fails by step 25k**, two cheap levers to pull next:
- **Single-house sanity ceiling**: train medium_pla_v1 only on house 11
  data, eval only on house 11. If the policy still cannot grasp on the
  house it was trained on, the bottleneck is the architecture / loss, not
  data diversity. ~1 h training.
- **Action-space probe**: plot per-trajectory gripper close frame in the
  training data. If demonstrator gripper-close frames are themselves
  clustered near 48, the policy is doing the right thing on the data we
  showed it, and the dataset is the problem rather than the architecture.
